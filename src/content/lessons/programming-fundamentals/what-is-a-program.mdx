Picture a chef at the peak of his craft. He's designed a five-course dinner — intricate, beautiful, exactly the meal he'd cook if he could. The problem: he's paralyzed. He can't stand at the stove. He can't hold a knife. He can't even stir a pot.

What he _can_ do is write.

So he puts together a crew of volunteers. Not professional cooks — most of them have never worked in a kitchen. But they're capable, they're willing, and they want to get this right. The chef knows they'll follow his instructions carefully. He also knows they'll follow them _literally_.

So he doesn't write "chop the onions." He writes:

> _Pick up the chef's knife with your dominant hand. Place the onion flat side down on the board. Make vertical cuts every quarter inch, then horizontal cuts. Pieces should be small and even._

He specifies everything — the tools, the timing, the order, what to do if something goes wrong. Every detail is written down because he knows his volunteers will do exactly what the list says. No more, no less. They won't fill in gaps. They won't use judgment they don't have.

That's not a flaw. That's exactly what makes them useful.

The volunteers aren't slow or confused — they're fast, capable, and genuinely trying to do a good job. What they don't have is intuition. They've never cooked before, so there's no way for them to fill in steps the chef might accidentally leave out.

If the instructions say "add the oil" before "heat the pan," the oil goes in cold. If the chef forgets salt, no one adds salt. If two steps are reversed, the dish comes out wrong — even though every volunteer followed the list faithfully.

A computer works the same way.

It reads your instructions from the top, one at a time, in the exact order you wrote them. It doesn't skip ahead. It doesn't decide a different order would work better. It doesn't fill in steps you forgot. It does what you wrote — and when something goes wrong, it's almost always because the instructions weren't quite right.

That's what a **program** is: a set of instructions you write for a computer to follow, in order, exactly as written.

And a **bug** is what happens when the chef writes something ambiguous, forgets a detail, or puts steps in the wrong order. The volunteers followed the list faithfully. The instructions were just wrong.

That's why programming is really about learning to think like the chef: carefully, precisely, always one step ahead of what your crew is about to do next.
